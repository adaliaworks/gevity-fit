The science

You don't have to overhaul
your life. The research
actually agrees.

There's a quiet finding buried in the longevity research that nobody puts on a poster: the amount of exercise that helps you live longer is far less than you've been led to believe — and far more ordinary. No two-hour workouts. No personality transplant. Just two simple habits, repeated for years.

Two habits. That's the protocol.

Strip away the supplements, the cold plunges, and the biohacking, and the evidence keeps pointing at the same two unglamorous things.

🚶

Move at an easy pace, regularly

Three brisk walks or moderate-effort bike rides a week gets most people where they need to be. The target is a pace where you can still hold a conversation, but wouldn't want to sing — often called "Zone 2."

🏋️

Lift something heavy-ish, briefly

Here's the part nobody tells you: the longevity payoff from strength training maxes out at roughly 30 to 60 minutes a week. A short, consistent routine isn't the compromise — it's the target.

The cardio side

Your aerobic fitness is a remarkably good predictor of how long you'll live.

In a large study of more than 120,000 people, cardiorespiratory fitness predicted all-cause mortality more strongly than traditional risk factors like high blood pressure, diabetes, or smoking.1Mandsager K, et al. JAMA Network Open, 2018 — cardiorespiratory fitness vs. all-cause mortality in 120,000+ adults. Put simply: how well your body uses oxygen is one of the clearest signals of long-term health we have.

The good news is that building it doesn't require athleticism. The fitness that matters most is built at an easy, sustainable pace — the kind you can keep up for thirty minutes while chatting with a friend. Brisk walking counts. So does an easy bike ride, a hike, or pushing a stroller up a hill.

Global health guidelines land in the same place: aim for about 150 minutes of moderate movement across the week.2World Health Organization — Guidelines on Physical Activity and Sedentary Behaviour, 2020. Three or four relaxed sessions gets most people there without a gym membership.

Talk, don't sing
If you can hold a conversation but couldn't comfortably belt out a song, you're in the zone that matters. No heart-rate monitor required.

The strength side

The benefit of lifting weights maxes out at under an hour a week.

This is the finding that should change how you think about the gym. A meta-analysis pooling 16 studies and over 260,000 people found that strength training was linked to a 15% lower risk of dying from any cause — and the benefit peaked at around 30 to 60 minutes per week.3Momma H, et al. British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2022 — meta-analysis of 16 studies, 260,000+ people.

More than that didn't help — and at very high volumes, the longevity benefit actually started to fade. A second large analysis found much the same: the sweet spot for living longer was around an hour a week, with diminishing returns beyond it.4Shailendra P, et al. American Journal of Preventive Medicine, 2022 — resistance training and mortality risk.

Read that again, because it's permission you've probably never been given: a short, consistent strength routine isn't a watered-down version of "real" training. For the purpose of a longer life, it is the goal.

The hard part isn't the workout. It's the for years.

Every study here measures habits sustained over time — not heroic months followed by burnout. The benefit comes from showing up at a reasonable dose, again and again, long after motivation fades.

The science

You don't have to overhaul
your life. The research
actually agrees.

There's a quiet finding buried in the longevity research that nobody puts on a poster: the amount of exercise that helps you live longer is far less than you've been led to believe — and far more ordinary. No two-hour workouts. No personality transplant. Just two simple habits, repeated for years.

Two habits. That's the protocol.

Strip away the supplements, the cold plunges, and the biohacking, and the evidence keeps pointing at the same two unglamorous things — and, as the research below shows, doing both together beats doing either one alone.

🚶

Move at an easy pace, regularly

Three brisk walks or moderate-effort bike rides a week gets most people where they need to be. The target is a pace where you can still hold a conversation, but wouldn't want to sing — often called "Zone 2."

🏋️

Lift something heavy-ish, briefly

Here's the part nobody tells you: the longevity payoff from strength training maxes out at roughly 30 to 60 minutes a week. A short, consistent routine isn't the compromise — it's the target.

The cardio side

Your aerobic fitness is a remarkably good predictor of how long you'll live.

In a study of more than 120,000 adults, low cardiorespiratory fitness carried a risk of early death at least as large as — and, among the least fit, larger than — traditional risk factors like heart disease, diabetes, or smoking.1Mandsager K, et al. JAMA Network Open, 2018 — in 122,007 adults, reduced fitness was "comparable to or greater than" coronary artery disease, diabetes, and smoking. A larger, more recent analysis of over 750,000 people found that reaching even moderate fitness — roughly what a 150-minutes-a-week habit builds — was associated with about half the risk of dying early.2Kokkinos P, et al. J Am Coll Cardiol, 2022 — fitness and mortality in 750,302 US veterans. Put simply: how well your body uses oxygen is one of the clearest signals of long-term health we have.

The good news is that building it doesn't require athleticism. The fitness that matters most is built at an easy, sustainable pace — the kind you can keep up for thirty minutes while chatting with a friend. Brisk walking counts. So does an easy bike ride, a hike, or pushing a stroller up a hill.

Global health guidelines land in the same place: at least 150 minutes of moderate movement a week (and up to 300 is better still), plus a couple of short strength sessions.3World Health Organization — Guidelines on Physical Activity and Sedentary Behaviour, 2020: at least 150–300 min moderate activity a week, plus muscle-strengthening on 2+ days. Three or four relaxed sessions gets most people there without a gym membership — and if you're starting from zero, the guidance is simply that every move counts: the biggest gains come from doing a little rather than nothing at all.

The strength side

The benefit of lifting weights maxes out at under an hour a week.

This is the finding that should change how you think about the gym. A meta-analysis pooling 16 studies and over 260,000 people found that strength training was linked to a 15% lower risk of dying from any cause — independent of any cardio — and the benefit peaked at around 30 to 60 minutes per week.4Momma H, et al. British Journal of Sports Medicine, 2022 — 16 studies, 260,000+ people; all-cause mortality RR 0.85. And the two habits compound: people who did both the weekly movement and a little strength work had roughly 40% lower risk of dying from any cause than people who did neither — more than either habit alone.4Momma H, et al., 2022 — combined muscle-strengthening + aerobic activity, all-cause mortality RR 0.60.

More than that didn't help — and at very high volumes, the longevity benefit actually started to fade. A second large analysis found much the same: the sweet spot for living longer was around an hour a week, with diminishing returns beyond it.5Shailendra P, et al. American Journal of Preventive Medicine, 2022 — resistance training and mortality; peak ~27% risk reduction near 60 min/week.

Read that again, because it's permission you've probably never been given: a short, consistent strength routine isn't a watered-down version of "real" training. For the purpose of a longer life, it is the goal.

The hard part isn't the workout. It's the for years.

Every study here measures habits sustained over time — not heroic months followed by burnout. The benefit comes from showing up at a reasonable dose, again and again, long after motivation fades.
